What Constant Contact Actually Gives You for $12 a Month

The Lite plan at $12 per month covers up to 500 contacts and includes unlimited email sends. That is the key detail most people miss: you are not paying per email sent. You pay based on how many contacts are in your list, not how many times you email them.

Here is what comes with the Lite plan:

  • Drag-and-drop email editor with over 200 professionally designed templates. You can build a polished-looking email in about 20 minutes even if you have never designed anything in your life.
  • Contact management with list segmentation so you can separate, say, customers who bought in the last 30 days from customers you have not heard from in six months.
  • Signup forms you can embed on your website or share as a link on social media — critical for growing your list organically.
  • Basic automation: a welcome email that fires automatically when someone joins your list. This alone is worth the monthly fee because it sets expectations and builds trust with new subscribers without you lifting a finger.
  • Real-time reporting: open rates, click rates, bounces, and unsubscribes. You know within hours whether your subject line landed or flopped.
  • Phone and chat support — a genuine differentiator. Many competitors at this price point give you nothing but a help center article.

Step up to the Standard plan at $35 per month and you add A/B subject line testing, scheduled sends, more advanced automations (like re-engagement sequences), and better segmentation tools. For most businesses just starting out, the Lite plan is the right entry point.

How to Get Real Results from Constant Contact in Your First 30 Days

Signing up is the easy part. Getting actual revenue out of the tool is what matters. Here is a practical 30-day plan that works for almost any small business.

Week 1: Set Up Your Foundation

Import your existing contacts from wherever they live — your point-of-sale system, a spreadsheet, your phone contacts, your Gmail. Constant Contact accepts CSV files and direct integrations with Shopify, Salesforce, and QuickBooks. Clean up obvious duplicates using the deduplication tool. Then set up your welcome email: a single message that introduces your business, tells subscribers what to expect (how often you will email, what kind of content), and offers something small — a 10% discount, a free tip sheet, a recipe. This email will fire automatically from here on out every time someone new joins your list.

Week 2: Send Your First Campaign

Pick a template that matches your industry — Constant Contact labels them by business type. Write a subject line that references something timely: a season, a local event, a product you just got in stock. Keep the body copy short: one main point, one image, one call to action. Send it on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning between 9 a.m. and 11 a.m. in your audience's time zone — this is when open rates are statistically highest. Check your open rate 24 hours later. Industry average for small business emails is around 21%. If you hit that or better, your list is healthy. If you fall below 15%, your subject lines need work.

Week 3: Segment and Personalize

Look at who opened your first campaign and who did not. Create two segments: openers and non-openers. Send a follow-up to non-openers with a different subject line and the same content — this alone can recover 10–15% of the opens you missed the first time. Tag your openers as "engaged." These are your most valuable contacts, and future campaigns should treat them differently.

Week 4: Set Up One Automation Beyond Welcome

On the Standard plan, build a simple two-step re-engagement sequence for contacts who have not opened an email in 90 days. Email one asks if they still want to hear from you. Email two, sent three days later if they did not open email one, offers a small incentive to come back. This keeps your list clean and your open rates healthy, which protects your sender reputation over the long haul.

Where Constant Contact Is Not the Best Choice

Honesty matters here. Constant Contact is not the right pick for every situation.

If you need a free forever plan: Constant Contact does not have one. The 60-day trial is generous, but after that you pay. If cash flow is extremely tight and you cannot commit $12 a month, Mailchimp's free tier (500 contacts, 1,000 sends per month) or Brevo's free tier (unlimited contacts, 300 emails per day) are legitimate starting points — just know you will outgrow them quickly.

If you run complex B2B sales funnels: Constant Contact's automation builder is good, not great. If you need branching multi-step sequences tied to CRM pipeline stages, lead scoring, and conditional logic across dozens of triggers, ActiveCampaign is the better tool — it is built for that use case. Expect to pay $29–$49 per month and invest real time in the setup.

If your primary channel is SMS: Constant Contact added SMS in recent years, but it is an add-on cost and lags behind dedicated SMS platforms like Klaviyo or Postscript for e-commerce. If SMS is your primary channel, start there and add email later.

For the vast majority of local service businesses, brick-and-mortar retailers, freelancers, consultants, and online sellers, none of these edge cases apply. Constant Contact does exactly what you need.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does Constant Contact cost for a small business?

Constant Contact's Lite plan starts at $12 per month for up to 500 contacts. The Standard plan starts at $35 per month and adds automation and A/B testing. Pricing scales with your contact count. There is a 60-day free trial so you can test the platform before committing to any paid plan.

Is Constant Contact better than Mailchimp for small businesses?

For most small businesses, yes. Constant Contact has better customer support, including live phone help, which Mailchimp removed from most paid tiers. Constant Contact also has stronger event management tools and list-building features. Mailchimp wins on free-tier generosity, but once you need support or grow past a basic list, Constant Contact pulls ahead.

What is the ROI of email marketing for small businesses?

Industry data consistently shows email marketing returns around $36 for every $1 spent. For a small business on Constant Contact's $12-per-month Lite plan, a single returning customer who spends more than $12 puts you in the green. Most campaigns to a warm list of even 200 contacts generate far more than that from the first send.

Does Constant Contact work for very small lists, like under 500 contacts?

Absolutely. The Lite plan is built for lists under 500 contacts and costs $12 per month. You get unlimited email sends, a drag-and-drop editor, signup forms, and basic reporting. A hair salon, a local bakery, or a freelance consultant with even 150 loyal customers can see real revenue results from a monthly newsletter at this price point.

Can I cancel Constant Contact at any time?

Yes. Constant Contact does not lock you into a long-term contract on its monthly billing option. You can cancel through your account dashboard or by calling their support line. If you prepay annually you receive a discount, but monthly billing gives you full flexibility to leave whenever you want with no cancellation fee.
