What Makes an Email Tool a Real Alternative — Not Just a Cheaper Copy

A genuine alternative has to match Constant Contact's core strengths, not just undercut its price. Before we rank the tools below, here are the five criteria we used to evaluate each one:

  1. Deliverability rate: What percentage of emails actually land in the inbox, not the spam folder. Anything below 95% is a red flag.
  2. Ease of use: Could a 55-year-old bakery owner who has never used email software figure it out in one afternoon? We tested every editor with real business owners.
  3. Automation quality: Can you set up a welcome series, a win-back campaign, and a birthday coupon without reading a manual?
  4. Support access: Is there a human being you can call when something goes wrong the night before your holiday sale campaign goes out?
  5. Total cost for 500–5,000 contacts: We calculated real monthly costs at three list sizes — 500, 1,000, and 5,000 contacts — because the advertised starting price rarely tells the full story.

We also looked at integration counts (how many third-party tools each platform connects to), landing page builders, and signup form quality, because those features matter to small businesses trying to grow their lists without a developer.

Each Alternative Explained in Plain Language

Mailchimp — Best for Designers Who Want Creative Freedom

Mailchimp has the largest template library of any tool on this list and a genuinely impressive creative editor. If you send highly visual campaigns — think fashion boutique, interior design studio, or food brand — Mailchimp gives you more design control than Constant Contact. The free plan covers up to 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month, which is enough to test the platform before committing.

The catches: no phone support at any price tier, and the free plan removes Mailchimp branding only on paid plans starting at $13 per month. Automation is solid at the standard tier, but you need the $20 per month Essentials plan to unlock multi-step journeys. Customer support relies on chat and email tickets, which means you might wait hours for help during a crisis.

Brevo — Best for Large Lists on a Tight Budget

Brevo charges by emails sent, not contacts stored. If you have 10,000 contacts but only email them once a month, Brevo can cost significantly less than a contact-based platform. The Starter plan at $9 per month includes 5,000 emails per month. Scale to 20,000 sends and you pay $18 per month, which is competitive.

The trade-offs: the interface is less polished than Constant Contact or Mailchimp, and the email editor requires more clicks to accomplish simple tasks. Phone support does not exist; you get email support on the paid plan and chat on higher tiers. Transactional email and SMS are baked in, which is a bonus if you need them.

ActiveCampaign — Best for Businesses That Have Outgrown Basic Automation

If you want to segment contacts based on website behavior, purchase history, email engagement score, and lead scoring — all in one workflow — ActiveCampaign is the tool for you. Its automation builder is genuinely best-in-class. You can create multi-branch if/then logic that would require a developer to replicate in most other tools.

The problem is complexity. ActiveCampaign has a steep learning curve. Most small business owners spend one to two weeks just understanding the interface before they send their first campaign. It starts at $15 per month for up to 1,000 contacts, but the plan that unlocks the best automation features starts at $49 per month. It is overkill for businesses with fewer than 2,000 contacts and a basic email strategy.

AWeber — The Closest Direct Alternative to Constant Contact

AWeber is the closest competitor to Constant Contact in terms of philosophy. Both tools prioritize simplicity, deliverability, and live support. AWeber has a free plan for up to 500 subscribers that includes basic email sends and one landing page. Paid plans start at $12.50 per month.

Where AWeber falls short: the template designs feel older than Constant Contact's, the automation builder is limited compared to even Mailchimp, and the reporting dashboard lacks the event marketing and social media integrations that Constant Contact has built out. If you specifically need a simple, supported alternative and Constant Contact is not available in your region, AWeber is a reasonable backup.

How to Switch Email Platforms Without Breaking Your List

If you have decided to move away from Constant Contact — or to move to it from another tool — here is a clean process that protects your list and your deliverability.

Step 1: Export Your Contacts as a CSV

Every platform covered in this article supports CSV imports and exports. Log into your current tool, navigate to the contacts or audience section, and export all contacts including their custom fields, tags, and subscription status. Save two copies — one to your desktop, one to cloud storage.

Step 2: Document Your Current Segments and Tags

Write down every segment you currently use, the logic behind it (for example: "Opened an email in the last 90 days AND is in the VIP tag"), and which campaigns use each segment. You will need to recreate these on the new platform, and your notes will save you an hour of reverse-engineering.

Step 3: Authenticate Your Domain on the New Platform

Before you send a single email from your new account, add the SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records that the new platform gives you to your domain's DNS settings. This step is what tells inbox providers like Gmail and Outlook that your emails are legitimate. Every platform has a help article walking you through this in about 15 minutes.

Step 4: Warm Up Your New Account

Do not blast your entire list on day one. Send to your most engaged segment first — people who opened an email from you in the last 60 days. Wait 48 hours, check your open and spam complaint rates, then expand to your next segment. Over two to four weeks, you build a positive sending reputation on the new infrastructure.

Step 5: Run Both Platforms in Parallel for Two Weeks

Keep your old account active during the transition. If something goes wrong with deliverability on the new platform, you can still reach your audience. Cancel the old account only after two full campaigns have sent successfully from the new tool and your open rates are matching historical averages.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Mailchimp a good Constant Contact alternative for beginners?

Mailchimp has a free plan and a friendly drag-and-drop editor, so it looks attractive to beginners. But once your list grows past 500 contacts, the price jumps fast and key automation features lock behind higher tiers. Constant Contact gives you phone support from day one, which most beginners actually need more than a free plan.

What is the cheapest Constant Contact alternative?

Brevo (formerly Sendinblue) has the lowest entry price because it charges by emails sent rather than contacts stored. If you have a large list but send infrequently, Brevo can cost less per month. That said, Constant Contact starts at just $12 per month for up to 500 contacts, which beats most alternatives at that contact level.

Which Constant Contact alternative has the best automation?

ActiveCampaign leads on automation depth, offering branching workflows, predictive sending, and CRM-level contact scoring. It is best for businesses that already have a solid list and want to segment aggressively. If you are just starting out, Constant Contact's automation is simpler to set up and good enough for the welcome-series and re-engagement campaigns most small businesses need.

Can I migrate my list from Constant Contact to another tool easily?

Yes. Every major platform covered here accepts a CSV export from Constant Contact. You export your contacts, import the CSV into the new tool, and map your custom fields. The whole process takes under 30 minutes for most lists. Tags and segments may need manual recreation, so document your current segment rules before you export.

Does switching email platforms hurt my deliverability?

A platform switch itself does not hurt deliverability, but a sudden spike in volume on a new sending domain can trigger spam filters. Warm up your new account by sending smaller batches over two to four weeks. Authenticate your domain with SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records on the new platform before your first send, and you will be fine.
