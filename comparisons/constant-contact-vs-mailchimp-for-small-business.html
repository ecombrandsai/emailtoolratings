Constant Contact vs Mailchimp: The Head-to-Head Breakdown

Let us go feature by feature. These are the things that actually matter when you are running a small business and do not have a dedicated marketing team.

Pricing

Constant Contact starts at $12 per month for up to 500 contacts on its Lite plan. The Standard plan, which includes automation and more robust analytics, starts at $35 per month. Pricing scales with your list size, which is standard across the industry.

Mailchimp has a free tier capped at 500 contacts and 1,000 email sends per month. Once you hit those limits or need features like A/B testing and advanced automation, you move to Essentials at $13 per month or Standard at $20 per month. At face value, Mailchimp looks cheaper. The problem is the free plan quietly removes things you will eventually need — like the ability to remove Mailchimp's logo from your emails, or to set up a proper welcome sequence.

For most small businesses sending to a list of 500–2,500 contacts, the actual monthly spend on either platform is comparable. The difference is what you get for that spend.

Ease of Use

Constant Contact was built with non-technical users in mind. The dashboard is clean, the email editor uses a genuine drag-and-drop interface, and the onboarding flow walks you through sending your first email in about 20 minutes. If you have ever built a PowerPoint slide, you can build a Constant Contact email.

Mailchimp has improved significantly over the years, but it still shows the seams of a product that has grown by adding features on top of features. New users frequently report confusion around the distinction between "campaigns" and "automations," and the template editor has quirks that frustrate people who are not used to web design tools.

Customer Support

This is where the gap becomes significant for small business owners. Constant Contact offers phone support on all paid plans. You can call a real person, describe your problem in plain English, and get help. They also offer live chat and an extensive knowledge base.

Mailchimp removed phone support for its lower-tier plans years ago. On the free plan, you get email support only for the first 30 days, then nothing. On Essentials, you get email and chat. You only get phone support if you are on the Premium plan, which starts at $350 per month. That is not a realistic option for most small businesses.

Email Deliverability

Deliverability is the percentage of your emails that actually land in your subscribers' inboxes rather than their spam folders. Independent benchmarking in 2025 showed Constant Contact averaging inbox placement of around 97%. Mailchimp came in at approximately 94%. Three percentage points sounds small, but if you are sending to 1,000 people, that is 30 extra subscribers missing your email entirely — every single time you send.

Automation

Both platforms offer automation on their paid plans. Constant Contact includes a straightforward welcome email series, birthday emails, and behavioural triggers. Mailchimp's automation on the Standard plan is more flexible and includes more branching logic, which is genuinely useful if you have the time and inclination to build complex flows.

For most small businesses — a bakery, a landscaping company, a local gym, a freelance consultant — the automation in Constant Contact covers 90% of what you will actually use. You do not need to send emails based on which exact product page someone visited. You need to send a welcome email, a follow-up after a purchase, and maybe a re-engagement email to people who have not opened in 90 days. Constant Contact handles all of that cleanly.

The Templates Are Genuinely Useful

Constant Contact has over 200 professionally designed email templates organised by industry and occasion. You can find a template for a retail sale, a restaurant promotion, a nonprofit fundraiser, or a service business appointment reminder — and each one is built to work on both desktop and mobile without any tweaking. How to Get the Most Out of Constant Contact From Day One

Setting up an account is only the first step. These practical tips will help you see results in your first 30 days.

1. Import Your Existing Contacts First

Before you design a single email, import your contacts. Constant Contact accepts CSV files, Excel spreadsheets, or direct imports from Gmail, Outlook, and Salesforce. If you are switching from Mailchimp, export your contacts as a CSV and upload them directly. Tag them by source — existing customers, newsletter sign-ups, event attendees — so you can segment from day one.

2. Set Up a Welcome Email Before Anything Else

Go to the Campaigns tab, click "Create," and select "Automated Email Series." Build a single welcome email that goes out automatically when someone joins your list. Keep it simple: thank them, tell them what to expect from your emails, and give them one reason to be glad they signed up — a discount code, a free resource, or just a warm personal note from the business owner. This single automation pays for the entire subscription by itself if you set it up correctly.

3. Send on Tuesdays or Thursdays at 10 a.m. Local Time

Constant Contact's own data and industry-wide studies consistently show that emails sent Tuesday through Thursday, between 9 a.m. and 11 a.m. local time, see the highest open rates. This is not a rule that works for every industry, but it is the right starting point while you gather your own data. After your first four campaigns, look at your own open rate trends in the Reports tab and adjust accordingly.

4. Keep Subject Lines Under 50 Characters

More than 60% of emails are opened on mobile devices, where long subject lines get cut off. Write subject lines that are specific, short, and lead with the value — "20% off this weekend only" is better than "We are pleased to inform you that we are running a limited-time promotion." Constant Contact shows you a preview of how your subject line will appear on mobile before you send.

5. Use the Resend-to-Non-Openers Feature

One of the most underused features in Constant Contact is the ability to resend a campaign to everyone who did not open it the first time, with a different subject line, 48 hours later. This single step typically adds 20–30% more opens to any given campaign. You will find it in the Reports section after your campaign has been sent for at least 24 hours.

The One Case Where Mailchimp Might Be the Right Call

This comparison would not be honest if it did not acknowledge where Mailchimp genuinely has an edge. If you are a solo operator, you are very technically comfortable, you have fewer than 500 contacts, and you have zero budget for marketing tools right now, Mailchimp's free plan lets you send up to 1,000 emails per month at no cost. That is a real advantage if cash flow is genuinely constrained.

Mailchimp's Standard plan automation editor is also more flexible than Constant Contact's for building complex multi-branch sequences — the kind you might want if you are running an online course business and need to segment users based on which module they completed. That level of automation complexity is real and useful, but it is also overkill for the vast majority of small businesses we speak to.

The moment you need to pick up the phone and ask someone a question, the moment your list grows past 500 contacts, or the moment you want your emails to look professional without spending an hour editing a template — Constant Contact becomes the better option.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Constant Contact better than Mailchimp for beginners?

Yes. Constant Contact is designed from the ground up for people who are not marketing experts. Its drag-and-drop editor is simpler, its onboarding is guided, and its phone support means you can call a real person when you get stuck. Mailchimp has a steeper learning curve and limits phone support to higher-tier plans that most small businesses would never pay for.

Does Mailchimp still have a free plan in 2026?

Mailchimp does offer a free plan capped at 500 contacts and 1,000 sends per month, but it removes key features like A/B testing, behavioural automation, and the ability to remove Mailchimp branding from your emails. For a growing small business, you will hit those limits fast and end up paying anyway — often for a plan that costs as much as Constant Contact but delivers less support.

Which tool has better email deliverability?

Both platforms maintain strong sender reputations, but independent tests in 2025 showed Constant Contact achieving inbox placement rates of around 97%, compared to Mailchimp's 94%. That 3-point gap can mean dozens or hundreds of missed opens for every campaign you send. If your emails do not land in the inbox, nothing else about the tool matters — your message simply never gets seen.

Can I migrate my list from Mailchimp to Constant Contact easily?

Yes. You export your contacts from Mailchimp as a CSV file and upload them directly into Constant Contact. The process takes about ten minutes. Constant Contact's support team will also walk you through it for free if you run into any issues. Your campaign history does not transfer, but your contacts and tags do — so your segments remain intact and you can start sending immediately.

What happens to my Mailchimp automations if I switch?

Your Mailchimp automations will stop sending once you move your list. You will need to recreate them in Constant Contact. The good news is that Constant Contact's automation builder uses a visual flow editor that most users find quicker to set up than Mailchimp's. Plan for about an hour to rebuild a basic welcome or follow-up sequence, and you will be up and running the same day you switch.
